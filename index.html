Artifact 1.1 · AIML-500 Workshop 1

Machine Learning Study Assistant

AI Lab · Design-Thinking Build

An AI Assistant That Teaches, Not Answers-For

A conversational study assistant I designed and built to help students learn core machine-learning concepts through plain-language explanations and quizzing — with guardrails that keep it a learning tool, not a shortcut.

Student: "Explain overfitting"
Assistant: "Think of a student who memorizes last year's exam answers instead of understanding the material — they ace the old test but fail new questions. An overfit model does the same…"
View the live assistant →

Introduction

This artifact is an AI-powered study assistant I created during the AIML-500 AI Lab using a structured design-thinking process. It represents my first hands-on experience turning a real user need into a working AI product.

Description

The Machine Learning Study Assistant is a conversational assistant that explains introductory machine-learning concepts — supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning, overfitting, neural networks, deep learning, and model evaluation such as accuracy, precision, and recall. It can also quiz a learner one question at a time and give feedback. By design, it declines to complete graded assignments and instead offers to help the student learn, and it asks clarifying questions rather than guessing when given too little information.

Objective

To give students new to machine learning a patient, on-demand study partner that makes dense concepts approachable and builds confidence — while modeling responsible, academic-integrity-friendly use of AI in education.

Process

  • Empathy: identified the real pain point — new learners find ML terminology dense and easily confuse concepts like precision vs. recall.
  • Define: framed a focused problem statement and deliberately limited scope to explaining and quizzing, not doing graded work.
  • Ideate: compared a flashcard bot, a concept explainer, and a quiz coach, then combined the explainer and quiz coach for the most learning value.
  • Prototype: wrote a detailed instruction set answering ten planning questions (objective, knowledge, guardrails, tone, flow, and success criteria).
  • Test & iterate: ran four scenarios; when an early version began drafting an assignment, I revised the instructions to refuse and redirect — an example of improving the product through testing.

Tools and Technologies Used

ChatGPT (GPT-4o) Design Thinking Prompt / Instruction Design STORM AI (research practice) Custom GPTs (practice)

Value Proposition

This assistant gives learners a judgment-free way to understand machine-learning concepts at their own pace, turning an intimidating subject into an approachable conversation — while demonstrating how AI can support education without undermining academic integrity.

Unique Value

Unlike a general chatbot, this assistant is built around learning guardrails: it refuses to do graded work, asks for clarification instead of guessing, and always points the learner back to their course material. That intentional restraint reflects the responsible-AI mindset I bring from a regulated, quality-driven background.

Relevance

For my audience — hiring teams and collaborators in data and AI roles — this artifact shows I can take a problem from empathy to a working, responsibly-designed AI solution, and explain my reasoning clearly to both technical and non-technical people.

The Evolution of Artificial Intelligence

Research · Visual Timeline

Seven Decades of AI, Mapped

Seven decades from a single provocative question — "Can machines think?" — to generative models that write, see, and reason. This nine-slide deck traces the milestones from the Dartmouth Workshop and Rosenblatt's Perceptron through two AI winters, AlexNet, Transformers, and ChatGPT — showing how hardware breakthroughs proved as decisive as the algorithms themselves.

Download slide deck (.pptx) →

Description

Structured as four eras — Foundations (1950–1979), the Expert-System Era (1980–1999), the Deep-Learning Boom (2000–2016), and the Generative Age (2017–now) — with two "AI winter" funding freezes marked along the way. Key milestones include the 1956 Dartmouth Workshop that named the field, Deep Blue's 1997 chess win, AlexNet's 2012 breakthrough, the 2017 Transformer paper, and ChatGPT's record-setting 2022 launch.
